This\ntrend is set to continue as the region\u2019s largest energy market,\n Germany , plans to source at least 35 percent of its power from\nsolar and wind by 2020, compared with 23 percent now.  German  day-ahead prices  ranged this year between a high of\n59.74 euros on Jan 22. and a low of minus 3.33 euros a megawatt-hour on June 15, on the Epex Spot SE exchange. Solar and  wind \ngeneration rose as high as 32.5 gigawatts on June 15, compared\nwith average output of 5.6 gigawatts over the past year, data\nfrom European Energy Exchange AG show.  Power price  volatility  rose to its highest since at least\n2007 in June, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.  Utility Cutbacks  The decline in  forward power  prices because of the growth\nin renewables output has reduced the earnings of German\nutilities. RWE AG, the country\u2019s second-largest utility, said in\nan earnings report on Nov. 14 that profit will drop by almost\nhalf next year as power prices fail to recover. EON, Germany\u2019s\nbiggest utility, said that 2013 profit will be at the lower end\nof its forecast range following a 52 percent slump in nine-month\nearnings.  EON cut staff at its global commodities unit, the\nproprietary trading, optimization and infrastructure businesses,\nby 33 percent this year to 1,468 as of Sept. 30, the company\nsaid in its third quarter earnings report published on Nov. 13.\n RWE (RWE)  plans to cut 10 percent of its workforce, or 6,750 jobs, by\n2016, the company said on Nov. 14.  JPMorgan Chase & Co. is said to be closing its energy-trading business in Geneva, cutting or relocating about 12 jobs,\na person with direct knowledge of the matter said on Nov. 15.\nThis follows Deutsche Bank AG, based in Frankfurt and Bank of\nAmerica Corp. in shutting some units trading commodities because\nof tighter regulation in Europe and the U.S. after the financial\ncrisis.  Changing Industry  \u201cThe whole industry is changing and needs to be adaptable.\nWe can add a lot of flexibility in a totally new environment,\u201d\nDanske Commodities\u2019 Nordal Clausen said.  Danske Commodities advises some power plant operators when\nto run in order to maximize profits, Nordal Clausen said. It\nalso sells power on behalf of renewable energy producers and\nadvises on feed-in tariffs and output management, according to\nthe company website.  The company has increased headcount by 73 percent since\nJanuary to 400 people, from 231 at the end of last year, company\nspokesman Simon Taylor said by e-mail on Nov. 19. Most recently,\nDanske Commodities hired in October Igor Ignjic from Axpo\nTrading U.K. as head of U.K. power and gas.  It boosted profit 77 percent in 2012, according to an\nearnings report published on April 19. Danske Commodities\nincreased its power trading outside exchanges 178 percent last\nyear as the share of renewable power in  Europe  rose.
