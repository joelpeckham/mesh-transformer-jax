The body of the article is as follows:\n    \nConfidence among U.S. homebuilders\nclimbed more than anticipated in February to the highest level\nsince May 2007, pointing to an improving outlook for\nconstruction.  The  National Association of Home Builders /Wells Fargo  index \nof builder confidence rose for a fifth straight month, to 29 in\nFebruary from 25 in January, figures from the Washington-based\ngroup showed today. The  median  forecast of economists surveyed\nby Bloomberg News called for a rise to 26. Readings below 50\nmean more respondents said conditions were poor.  D.R. Horton Inc. (DHI)  is among builders reporting a pickup in\ndemand as borrowing costs hover near a record low and hiring\naccelerates. Efforts by the Obama administration and the  Federal\nReserve  to shore up the real estate market and help distressed\nhomeowners may limit the supply of foreclosed houses, lifting\nprospects for the industry.  \u201cThe housing market is moving toward more sustainable\ngrowth,\u201d Barry Rutenberg, chairman of the National Association\nof Home Builders and a builder from Gainesville,  Florida , said\nin a statement. At the same time, the housing sector remains\nfragile, he said.  The last time the builder confidence index increased five\nstraight months or more was April through October 1995. February\nestimates of 51 economists in the Bloomberg survey ranged from\n23 to 28.  The gauge, which was first published in January 1985,\naveraged 54 in the five years leading to the recession in\nDecember 2007. It reached a record low of 8 in January 2009.  The builders group\u2019s index of current single-family  home\nsales  rose to 30 this month, the highest since May 2007, from 25\nin the prior month, today\u2019s report showed.  Sales Expectations  A measure of  sales expectations  for the next six months\nadvanced to 34, the best reading since July 2007, from 29. The\ngauge of buyer traffic rose to 22, the highest since June 2007,\nfrom 21.  The confidence survey asks builders to characterize current\nsales as \u201cgood,\u201d \u201cfair\u201d or \u201cpoor\u201d and to gauge prospective\nbuyers\u2019 traffic. It also asks participants to gauge the outlook\nfor the next six months.  Builders in the West led the increase, with the index\njumping to 44, the highest since July 2006, from 21. Confidence\nrose to 30 in the  Midwest (USHBMWMK) , the highest since April 2006.\nSentiment decreased in the Northeast and South.  D.R. Horton , the largest U.S. homebuilder by volume,\nreported net home orders rose to 3,794 in final three months of\n2011, from 3,363 a year earlier.  \u201cSimply put, our business feels more positive,\u201d Donald Tomnitz, chief executive officer of the Fort Worth, Texas-based\ncompany, said in a Jan. 27 conference call. At the same time,\n\u201cmacroeconomic and housing conditions remain soft,\u201d and \u201cwe\nare cautiously optimistic for the remainder for 2012,\u201d he said.  Foreclosure Practices  Bank of America Corp., JPMorgan Chase & Co. and three other\nU.S. banks reached a $25 billion settlement with 49 states and\nthe U.S. government to end a probe of abusive foreclosure\npractices prompted by the collapse of the housing price bubble.\nPresident  Barack Obama  said the agreement, announced on Feb. 9,\nis a \u201cmajor step\u201d in reviving the housing market.  Housing  starts  also are improving. Builders broke ground on\nnew homes at a 675,000 annual pace in January, up from a 657,000\nrate the previous month, according to the Bloomberg survey\nmedian ahead of a Commerce Department report due tomorrow.  Work on apartment projects has climbed as the foreclosure\ncrisis turned more Americans into renters, helping to reenergize\nthe industry that\u2019s struggled since triggering the last\nrecession.  Construction of multifamily units will lead homebuilding\nagain this year, allowing housing to contribute to growth for\nthe first time in seven years, according to forecasts from\neconomists Michelle Meyer of Bank of America Corp. and Celia\nChen of Moody\u2019s Analytics Inc.
